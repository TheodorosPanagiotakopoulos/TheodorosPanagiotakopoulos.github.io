  • This project addresses a fundamental challenge in semiconductor fabrication, which is understanding and predicting the behavior of metal deposition on semiconductor surfaces. Specifically, I examined the epitaxial growth of lead (Pb) on the germanium (Ge(111)) surface, an important process for creating reliable electronic contacts in integrated circuits. My goal was to determine the critical coverage at which Pb begins to form crystalline islands, a key factor that influences device performance and reliability. I began by conducting Density Functional Theory (DFT) simulations, enhanced using the DFT+U+J method to address the limitations of standard approximations in reproducing the electronic structure of Ge. By tuning the Hubbard U and exchange J parameters, I achieved an accurate band gap of 0.67 eV for Ge, closely matching experimental values. These high-precision simulations allowed me to calculate the chemical potential of Pb across a range of coverages. The results showed a clear threshold at 1.33 monolayers (ML), where the system energetically favors island formation over uniform film growth, marking the onset of nucleation. To extend these findings to realistic, large-scale systems where DFT becomes computationally expensive, I developed a two-stage machine learning framework. In the first stage, I trained a neural network model to predict atomic charges using data from 10,000 relaxed atomic configurations. Each configuration, containing 50 Ge and 10 Pb atoms, was represented using a characteristic matrix capturing spatial coordinates, atomic types, and local atomic environments. This model achieved strong generalization performance with a mean squared error (MSE) of 0.51 on the test set. In the second stage, I trained another neural network model to predict system energies using the charges predicted by the first model. I processed the inputs into a compact vector format and trained the network using cross-validation. The best model architecture featured layers of 128 and 64 neurons, with dropout to prevent overfitting, and achieved an MSE of 0.41 on unseen data. To scale the predictions to larger systems, I implemented a sliding window approach that enabled localized predictions while preserving the integrity of the full system’s structure. The results from my machine learning models closely aligned with the DFT simulations and experimental observations, particularly in identifying the nucleation threshold at 1.33 ML. Notably, the chemical potential curves produced by the ML models were smooth and consistent, further confirming the validity of the approach. This strong agreement demonstrated that machine learning can serve as a computationally efficient surrogate for traditional quantum simulations when modeling large systems. Overall, I developed a scalable and accurate methodology for predicting nucleation phenomena in metal-semiconductor systems. This is particularly relevant to the semiconductor industry, where alloys are increasingly used due to their enhanced performance. Since alloys often lack periodic boundaries, conventional simulations often fall short. By combining DFT with machine learning, I created a powerful tool for predicting optimal deposition conditions, potentially accelerating the design of next-generation semiconductor devices. This accomplishment played a pivotal role in securing NSF funding.

  • In this project, we aimed to predict the structural image of a metal (Pb) deposited on a semiconductor (Ge) surface, effectively visualizing the output of the deposition simulation. This is a complex task involving spatial data, which is well-suited for Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) due to their strength in detecting spatial features and patterns in grid-like data. CNNs allow for the learning of hierarchical representations, making them ideal for modeling the formation of atomic structures in three-dimensional space. We utilized 10,000 previously relaxed configurations, each containing 60 atoms (50 Ge and 10 Pb). Each configuration was embedded within a three-dimensional 10×10×10 voxel grid to spatially map atom positions and features. This resulted in a volumetric data structure representing the system, where each voxel stores six channels of information: atom type (one-hot encoded into four channels), atomic charge (normalized), and electrostatic potential (standardized). The data was organized into tensors of shape 10×10×10×6, which serve as the model input, while the target output for each sample is a 60×3 matrix representing the relaxed (x, y, z) positions of all atoms. To develop a predictive model, we applied extensive hyperparameter tuning and training optimization. Different CNN architectures were explored, testing 2 to 4 convolutional layers with filter sizes ranging from 16 to 128 and kernel sizes of 3×3×3 and 5×5×5. Pooling strategies (max and average pooling), batch normalization, and dropout were used to improve generalization and stabilize training. The feature extraction layers were followed by one or two dense layers for final regression. Optimizers including Adam, RMSprop, and SGD were evaluated, alongside learning rate schedules and varying batch sizes. The input features were normalized while atomic positions remained unscaled. The dataset was split into 80% training and 20% validation, with early stopping and learning rate adjustment applied to prevent overfitting and improve convergence. Our best-performing model begins with a 10×10×10×6 input tensor and passes it through two 3D convolutional layers with 32 and 64 filters, respectively, each using a 3×3×3 kernel. Batch normalization and max pooling follow each convolutional layer to downsample and regularize the learning process. After feature extraction, the output is flattened and passed through a dense hidden layer with 64 neurons and dropout (0.2–0.3) before the final dense layer outputs the predicted (x, y, z) positions of each atom. This model achieved the lowest validation loss using the Adam optimizer (learning rate ≈ 10⁻³), confirming its ability to generalize well. To prepare large systems for prediction, each Ge atom was treated as a reference point, and the surrounding space was voxelized into a 10×10×10 grid. Features from nearby 49 Ge and 10 Pb atoms were collected and encoded into a 1000×6 matrix, which was then reshaped into a 4D input tensor. This process was repeated for all atoms, resulting in a dynamic tensor that could be fed into the CNN. The final prediction for each atom’s position was calculated by averaging its predicted coordinates across all voxel windows in which it appeared. After generating predicted atomic positions, we compared them with those obtained via DFT relaxation. The resulting deposition images from both methods showed strong visual and structural agreement, confirming the CNN’s ability to accurately reproduce the relaxed atomic arrangements. Notably, this predictive capability scales to much larger systems where traditional simulations become computationally prohibitive. In conclusion, by integrating a Convolutional Neural Network into our modeling workflow, we created a highly effective method for predicting the final deposition pattern of metals on semiconductor surfaces. This model captures the complex spatial relationships and physical interactions within the system, offering a fast, scalable, and physically grounded alternative to conventional relaxation methods for large-scale atomic systems. This model significantly accelerated computational calculations, reducing the need for time-consuming simulations. Instead, it produces highly accurate results by multiplying the model’s learned weights with the input tensors, effectively reconstructing the atomic positions. These predicted coordinates are then used to generate morphology images that closely resemble those obtained from traditional DFT-based relaxation.

  • I introduced a novel method to model the electrolyte at the electrochemical interface using ab initio simulations of charge transfer processes at surfaces. I presented a simple capacitor model of the interface that addresses key challenges, including: i) the requirement for large cell heights to achieve convergence, which incurs significant computational costs, and ii) the costly iterative calculations of reaction energetics needed to tune the surface charge to the desired potential. I derived a correction to the energy for finite cell heights, enabling the calculation of large cell energies without additional computational expense. Furthermore, I demonstrated that reaction energetics determined at constant charge can be easily mapped to those at constant potential, eliminating the need for iterative schemes to tune the system to a constant potential. This work provided an efficient and accurate approach to modeling electrochemical interfaces.

  • I investigated the cation effect using small quaternary ammonium cations with different sizes and symmetries. For Bi-catalyzed CO2 Reduction Reaction (CO2RR), which produces CO and formate, I performed density functional theory (DFT) calculations and ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations to examine the effect of asymmetric cations (such as CH3NH3+) on CO2 adsorption and activation. My findings were supported by experimental studies, which confirmed that asymmetric cations have a stronger promotional effect on CO2RR activity compared to symmetric ones. This effect is attributed to the asymmetric and weak hydration shell that stabilizes adsorbed CO2. Additionally, by comparing cations of different sizes (NH4+, Me4N+, and Et4N+), I observed a significant effect of cation size on CO production activity, with negligible impact on formate production. My work further elucidated the critical effects of cation symmetry and size on CO2RR and proposed a method to improve electrocatalysis through optimized electrolytes. This research led to the publication of a paper.

My master's integrates deep learning methodologies to unravel the mysteries of supersymmetry and dark matter. The Standard Model, foundational to modern particle physics, falls short in addressing the hierarchy problem—the disparity between electroweak and gravitational forces. Supersymmetry, a hypothetical extension of the Standard Model, emerges as a compelling solution to this conundrum. Central to my research is the exploration of the Lightest Supersymmetric Particle (LSP), a theoretical entity holding profound implications for dark matter. Employing advanced deep learning techniques, I calculate the cross-section for the scattering of dark matter particles from ordinary matter, illuminating the elusive nature of dark matter. My investigation extends to both direct and indirect methods of dark matter detection, involving a thorough exploration of experimental setups and rigorous comparisons between theoretical predictions and observational astronomy data. Utilizing data science methodologies, I validate the robustness of the employed model, ensuring its alignment with real-world observations. In the realm of direct dark matter detection, I developed and trained a deep neural network specifically designed to identify the cross-section of dark matter particles, utilizing collected data from Fermi-LAT. The intricate workings of this neural network enhance the precision and reliability of our understanding of dark matter interactions. Moreover, my exploration extends to indirect methods of dark matter detection, including scenarios of annihilated dark matter particles and the observation of neutrinos and photons. In this context, another neural network I developed plays a crucial role, confirming the exclusive origins of neutrinos from specific particles. Throughout this scientific odyssey, these deep neural networks stand as pivotal tools within the overarching data science framework, refining and validating theoretical predictions against observational data from the vast expanse of the cosmos. My research thus culminates in a comprehensive understanding of the intricate interplay between supersymmetry and dark matter, with data science emerging as the indispensable tool for unraveling these cosmic enigmas.
