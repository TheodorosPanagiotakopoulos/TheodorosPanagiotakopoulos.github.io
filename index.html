  • I developed a data processing and visualization framework to evaluate machine learning models against rigorous simulations. In ASML’s workflow the optimizer does not see the validation data during training, most likely because of computational cost and efficiency constraints, since validating at every training step would significantly increase runtime and memory usage. As a result, an independent framework to evaluate model performance was needed. I designed pipelines that load intermediate model checkpoint results (modelchk_*, saved every few thousand steps) together with a rigorous reference (rigAI), align them by gauge, apply scenario-specific filters depending on whether the simulation is EUV or DUV, and compute physically meaningful error metrics. A key metric is the RMSE of the aerial image, which directly reflects optical model fidelity and is sensitive to overfitting, since a model that memorizes training data fails to generalize across slits, groups, and queueTags and therefore exhibits elevated validation RMSE. The pipelines separate training and validation data, compute aerial image RMSE and Model_CD RMSE across slits, groups, and queueTags, and aggregate the results into compact summary tables. For any set S of samples and a column c, the RMSE is defined as RMSEc = sqrt((1/|S|) Σi∈S (MLc,i − Rigc,i)²), which quantifies the deviation of the machine learning model from the rigorous reference in a physically interpretable way. I designed the framework with a two-layer parallelization approach. For a single simulation path, where the code must access and process many intermediate models saved at different steps, I used ThreadPoolExecutor, which was ideal for I/O-bound operations because multithreading allowed overlapping of frequent read and merge tasks without waiting for each access to complete. For larger-scale processing across multiple categories such as per group, per slit, and per queueTag, I used ProcessPoolExecutor, which is suited for CPU-intensive computations on large DataFrames and ensures that each CPU core works independently without being constrained by Python’s Global Interpreter Lock. By separating I/O-heavy and CPU-heavy tasks into different execution layers, the framework achieved both speed and stability, whereas alternative designs led to resource contention and bottlenecks. I also implemented Python code to export structured CSV results and generate high-resolution plots. These plots visualize training and validation loss curves, making it straightforward to identify cases of overfitting or underfitting. By combining automated data handling with precise statistical computations, I reduced manual effort, improved reproducibility, and delivered a framework that was later adopted by my team for large-scale simulation and machine learning experiments.

  • This project addresses a fundamental challenge in semiconductor fabrication, which is understanding and predicting the behavior of metal deposition on semiconductor surfaces. Specifically, I examined the epitaxial growth of lead (Pb) on the germanium (Ge(111)) surface, an important process for creating reliable electronic contacts in integrated circuits. My goal was to determine the critical coverage at which Pb begins to form crystalline islands, a key factor that influences device performance and reliability. I began by conducting Density Functional Theory (DFT) simulations, enhanced using the DFT+U+J method to address the limitations of standard approximations in reproducing the electronic structure of Ge. By tuning the Hubbard U and exchange J parameters, I achieved an accurate band gap of 0.67 eV for Ge, closely matching experimental values. These high-precision simulations allowed me to calculate the chemical potential of Pb across a range of coverages. The results showed a clear threshold at 1.33 monolayers (ML), where the system energetically favors island formation over uniform film growth, marking the onset of nucleation. To extend these findings to realistic, large-scale systems where DFT becomes computationally expensive, I developed a two-stage machine learning framework. In the first stage, I trained a neural network model to predict atomic charges using data from 10,000 relaxed atomic configurations. Each configuration, containing 50 Ge and 10 Pb atoms, was represented using a characteristic matrix capturing spatial coordinates, atomic types, and local atomic environments. This model achieved strong generalization performance with a mean squared error (MSE) of 0.51 on the test set. In the second stage, I trained another neural network model to predict system energies using the charges predicted by the first model. I processed the inputs into a compact vector format and trained the network using cross-validation. The best model architecture featured layers of 128 and 64 neurons, with dropout to prevent overfitting, and achieved an MSE of 0.41 on unseen data. To scale the predictions to larger systems, I implemented a sliding window approach that enabled localized predictions while preserving the integrity of the full system’s structure. The results from my machine learning models closely aligned with the DFT simulations and experimental observations, particularly in identifying the nucleation threshold at 1.33 ML. Notably, the chemical potential curves produced by the ML models were smooth and consistent, further confirming the validity of the approach. This strong agreement demonstrated that machine learning can serve as a computationally efficient surrogate for traditional quantum simulations when modeling large systems. Overall, I developed a scalable and accurate methodology for predicting nucleation phenomena in metal-semiconductor systems. This is particularly relevant to the semiconductor industry, where alloys are increasingly used due to their enhanced performance. Since alloys often lack periodic boundaries, conventional simulations often fall short. By combining DFT with machine learning, I created a powerful tool for predicting optimal deposition conditions, potentially accelerating the design of next-generation semiconductor devices. This accomplishment played a pivotal role in securing NSF funding.

  • In this project, I aimed to predict the structural image of a metal (Pb) deposited on a semiconductor (Ge) surface, effectively visualizing the output of the deposition simulation. This was a complex task involving spatial data, which is well-suited for Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) due to their strength in detecting spatial features and patterns in grid-like data. CNNs allow for the learning of hierarchical representations, making them ideal for modeling the formation of atomic structures in three-dimensional space. I utilized 10,000 previously relaxed configurations, each containing 60 atoms (50 Ge and 10 Pb). I embedded each configuration within a three-dimensional 10×10×10 voxel grid to spatially map atom positions and features. This resulted in a volumetric data structure representing the system, where each voxel stores six channels of information: atom type (one-hot encoded into four channels), atomic charge (normalized), and electrostatic potential (standardized). I organized the data into tensors of shape 10×10×10×6 to serve as model inputs, while the target output for each sample was a 60×3 matrix representing the relaxed (x, y, z) positions of all atoms. To develop a predictive model, I applied extensive hyperparameter tuning and training optimization. I explored different CNN architectures, testing 2 to 4 convolutional layers with filter sizes ranging from 16 to 128 and kernel sizes of 3×3×3 and 5×5×5. I used pooling strategies (max and average pooling), batch normalization, and dropout to improve generalization and stabilize training. After feature extraction, I added one or two dense layers for final regression. I evaluated optimizers including Adam, RMSprop, and SGD, along with learning rate schedules and various batch sizes. I normalized the input features while keeping atomic positions unscaled. I split the dataset into 80% training and 20% validation, using early stopping and learning rate adjustments to prevent overfitting and improve convergence. My best-performing model began with a 10×10×10×6 input tensor and passed it through two 3D convolutional layers with 32 and 64 filters, respectively, each using a 3×3×3 kernel. I applied batch normalization and max pooling after each convolution to downsample and regularize the learning process. The output was then flattened and passed through a dense hidden layer with 64 neurons and dropout (0.2–0.3), followed by a final dense layer that output the predicted (x, y, z) positions of each atom. This model achieved the lowest validation loss using the Adam optimizer (learning rate ≈ 10⁻³), confirming its strong generalization performance. To prepare large systems for prediction, I treated each Ge atom as a reference point and voxelized the surrounding space into a 10×10×10 grid. I collected features from nearby 49 Ge and 10 Pb atoms and encoded them into a 1000×6 matrix, which I reshaped into a 4D input tensor. I repeated this process for all atoms, generating a dynamic tensor to be fed into the CNN. I calculated the final prediction for each atom’s position by averaging its predicted coordinates across all voxel windows in which it appeared. After generating the predicted atomic positions, I compared them with those obtained from DFT relaxation. The resulting deposition images from both methods showed strong visual and structural agreement, confirming the CNN’s ability to accurately reproduce the relaxed atomic arrangements. Notably, this predictive capability scaled to much larger systems, where traditional simulations become computationally prohibitive. In conclusion, by integrating a Convolutional Neural Network into my modeling workflow, I developed a highly effective method for predicting the final deposition pattern of metals on semiconductor surfaces. My model captured the complex spatial relationships and physical interactions within the system, offering a fast, scalable, and physically grounded alternative to conventional relaxation methods for large-scale atomic systems. This model significantly accelerated computational calculations, reducing the need for time-consuming simulations. Instead, it produced highly accurate results by multiplying the model’s learned weights with the input tensors, effectively reconstructing the atomic positions. These predicted coordinates were then used to generate morphology images that closely resembled those obtained from traditional DFT-based relaxation.

  • I initiated a collaboration between the Department of Physics and the Department of Statistics and led the development of a machine learning methodology to improve node classification on incomplete graph datasets. The core challenge I addressed was how to maintain high prediction accuracy when parts of the data, specifically nodes, are missing. I set out to understand how different node removal strategies impact model performance, and how combining results from multiple models could enhance generalization across sparsely observed graphs. To tackle this, I used Simplified Graph Convolutional Networks (SGC), a lightweight and efficient alternative to traditional GCNs. SGC collapses multiple layers into a single propagation step and removes non-linearities, which allowed me to significantly reduce training time without sacrificing model accuracy. I implemented the entire pipeline from scratch in Julia, using the Flux ML library. I designed and engineered three custom graph reduction techniques: truncating the last n nodes, randomly removing a percentage of nodes, and eliminating a contiguous block of nodes. After each reduction, I recalculated the normalized adjacency matrix (S matrix) by incorporating self-loops and applying degree normalization, raising the result to the power of three to enable higher-order propagation. I trained the models using a one-shot learning approach, running a single forward pass on each reduced graph, followed by a simple single-layer neural network trained with cross-entropy loss and the Adam optimizer. To evaluate the model's effectiveness, I split the data into 60% training and 40% testing sets, and compared predicted vs. true labels using one-hot encodings. I carefully monitored accuracy on both reduced and full graphs. Notably, I introduced a parameter averaging strategy to further boost performance: by training separate models on slightly different graph reductions and averaging their learned weight matrices, I was able to significantly improve results when the final model was applied back to the full dataset. This approach consistently led to improved accuracy, rising from around 91% to as high as 94%, particularly when the node removal followed a structured, rather than random, pattern. I validated the method not only on the Cora citation dataset, but also on synthetic graphs generated using the Barabási–Albert model, which mimics the scale-free structure of many real-world networks. The pipeline scaled effectively, showing that this methodology can generalize well to unseen or non-periodic graphs. Overall, this project combined theoretical insights, practical engineering, and rigorous experimentation to address a real-world challenge in graph-based machine learning. It was a rewarding opportunity to lead a technically deep initiative and contribute to interdisciplinary research at the intersection of physics, statistics, network science, and neural networks.

  • In my recent research, I explored the Hydrogen Evolution Reaction (HER), a key process in water splitting and clean energy conversion. HER can proceed through different mechanisms depending on the electrolyte’s pH, but under neutral conditions, the Volmer step, where a hydrogen atom adsorbs onto the electrode surface, is the rate-limiting step. Because of this, my work specifically focuses on understanding and improving this critical part of the reaction. In a joint experimental and computational study, I discovered that non-metallic cations, particularly ammonium (NH₄⁺) and methylammonium (CH₃NH₃⁺), significantly enhance HER activity on a gold (Au(111)) surface compared to the metallic sodium ion (Na⁺). These findings raised an important question: what makes non-metallic cations more effective at promoting HER? To answer this, I employed Grand Canonical Density Functional Theory (GC-DFT), using the TPOT package in conjunction with VASP and VASPsol. This method allows the number of electrons to adjust dynamically during the simulation to maintain a constant electrode potential. I also used a realistic solvent model with 40 water molecules and a 4×4×5 Au(111) slab to simulate the electrolyte environment, and applied a slow-growth approach to calculate the activation barriers of the reaction. Through these simulations, I found that hydrogen can adsorb on the electrode surface via two general pathways: from the dissociation of water molecules within the cation’s hydration shell or from bulk water outside of it. However, in systems with non-metallic cations, I identified two additional mechanisms: direct cation splitting and proton shuttling. These unique pathways lower the energy barrier for hydrogen adsorption, helping to accelerate the HER. Overall, my results show that the identity of the cation plays a critical role in the HER mechanism and efficiency. Non-metallic cations like NH₄⁺ and CH₃NH₃⁺ not only lower activation barriers, but also introduce new proton transfer routes not available with traditional metal cations. This insight could open up new directions for catalyst design in future energy technologies.

  • This project investigates key challenges in sustainable electrochemistry, focusing on how electrolyte composition, particularly the nature of cations, influences the electrochemical reduction of carbon dioxide (CO₂RR). My work centered on Bi(111), a promising catalyst for CO₂ conversion, and how non-metal cations affect CO₂ adsorption and product formation. The aim was to understand the mechanistic role of these cations and determine how they modulate binding energies, activation barriers, and reaction pathways that govern the generation of CO and formate. To pursue this, I designed and conducted a series of high-fidelity simulations using grand canonical density functional theory (GC-DFT). I employed the TPOT package alongside VASP and VASPSol, which allowed me to maintain fixed electrode potentials by dynamically optimizing the system's electron count. This method was essential for accurately capturing the electrochemical environment and isolating potential-dependent phenomena in a controlled and reproducible manner. I systematically explored a range of non-metallic cations—NH₄⁺, CH₃NH₃⁺, and (CH₃)₄N⁺—and benchmarked them against a conventional metallic cation, Na⁺. Through detailed GC-DFT calculations, I evaluated CO₂ binding energies and found that non-metal cations significantly enhanced CO₂ adsorption by up to 0.2 eV compared to Na⁺. This enhancement was attributed to stronger electrostatic interactions and more favorable charge transfer characteristics at the Bi–CO₂ interface. In addition, I mapped the full reaction landscape for CO₂ reduction, from initial adsorption to the formation of CO and HCOO⁻. The study revealed that formate can be produced via two distinct pathways—direct hydrogenation of solvated CO₂, or surface adsorption followed by hydrogenation. The former pathway emerged as energetically preferred and largely independent of the cation, while CO formation was more sensitive to cation identity and hydration dynamics. To explore the role of water and proton availability, I carried out nudged elastic band (NEB) calculations for water dissociation and hydrogen shuttling, revealing activation barriers of 1.10–1.12 eV for Na⁺ and NH₄⁺. These steps are critical in delivering reactive hydrogen species to CO₂, enabling the formation of intermediates such as COOH* and ultimately CO. The results showed that NH₄⁺ uniquely stabilizes these intermediates through hydrogen bonding, which contributes to lower desorption barriers and improved CO₂RR activity. This work presents a comprehensive model of how non-metal cations interact with both the electrode and reaction intermediates. The study demonstrated that non-metal cations often outperform metal cations by enhancing CO₂ adsorption and improving the energetics of downstream transformations. These insights offer practical guidance for the design of next-generation electrolytes and catalytic interfaces aimed at carbon-neutral fuel synthesis. Overall, this research illustrates how grand canonical DFT, combined with mechanistic insight and electrochemical understanding, can bridge the gap between atomic-scale theory and experimentally observable behavior. The findings have supported key experimental collaborations and helped secure Department of Energy funding, while laying a strong foundation for future data-driven modeling of electrochemical interfaces.

This thesis develops a unified computational framework that integrates first-principles modeling with machine learning to address complex, multi-scale problems in energy conversion, materials growth, and graph-structured data. A central challenge across these domains is to extract reliable predictions from incomplete or computationally intensive datasets and apply them to larger, realistic systems. The research focuses on combining physical insight with scalable learning architectures to model atomic-level interactions, thin film growth, and data-limited environments.

The work begins with an investigation into thin film nucleation in metal-semiconductor systems, specifically the epitaxial growth of Pb on Ge(111). High-accuracy DFT+U+J calculations revealed a critical nucleation threshold at 1.33 monolayers, beyond which island formation becomes energetically favorable. To extend this understanding to non-periodic, large-scale systems, a two-stage machine learning surrogate model was developed. One neural network predicted atomic charges, while another estimated total system energies, achieving results consistent with DFT and enabling efficient exploration of alloy deposition conditions.

To visualize the structural outcomes of deposition, a 3D convolutional neural network (CNN) was constructed to predict relaxed atomic positions from initial configurations. Using voxelized tensors encoding atom types, charges, and electrostatic potentials, the CNN learned spatial relationships governing final morphology. The model generated atomistic structures nearly indistinguishable from DFT-relaxed geometries, offering a fast, scalable alternative for simulating complex deposition processes.

Following this, the thesis explores how data from first-principles simulations can be further abstracted into graph representations to enable analysis under sparse conditions. In collaboration with the Departments of Physics and Statistics, a methodology was developed to encode atomic configurations or simulation domains as graphs, allowing truncated or reduced-node simulations. Using Simplified Graph Convolutional Networks (SGCs) implemented in Julia, the project introduced three node-reduction strategies and a parameter averaging technique to enhance robustness across sparsely observed graphs. Applied back to the full systems, this ensemble approach increased node classification accuracy from 91% to 94%. Validated on the Cora citation network and Barabási–Albert synthetic graphs, the method demonstrated strong generalization performance and parallels the scalability challenges encountered in quantum modeling.

The final section turns to sustainable electrochemical systems, focusing on the CO₂ reduction reaction (CO₂RR) and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER). To simulate these reactions under constant electrode potential, two key tools were developed: a hybrid solvation model (SOLHYBRID) and a potential control algorithm (TPOT). These enabled grand-canonical DFT and AIMD simulations that uncovered how non-metallic ammonium cations like CH₃NH₃⁺ enhance CO₂ adsorption and reduce the activation barrier for H* formation on bismuth electrodes. Experimental validation using H-cell and gas-diffusion electrodes (GDEs) confirmed that CH₃NH₃⁺ achieves nearly double the current density compared to Na⁺.

Simulations of HER on Au(111) surfaces revealed novel cation-mediated mechanisms, including proton shuttling and direct proton donation, that are inaccessible to traditional metallic cations. These pathways reduce energy barriers and establish ammonium-based species as active co-catalysts in electrochemical reactions.

Together, these studies demonstrate how machine learning can extend the reach of first-principles simulations, from predicting atomic configurations to learning from incomplete data. By unifying techniques across surface science, electrochemistry, and graph theory, this work provides scalable, physically grounded models that support the design of next-generation materials, interfaces, and sustainable energy technologies.
